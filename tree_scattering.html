key facts (how to read this picture)
  • A ray direction ⇔ a contact pair (p,q). The curves on that ray form a degree column dL−mE with m = 3d−P−Q, and every unicurve pairing is linear in d.
  • So the existence filter is a window in d: max(lower bounds) ≤ d ≤ min(upper bounds). Each unicurve caps from one side only: upper-type if 3m > d, lower-type if 3m < d.
  • Cones = maximal ranges where the upper anchor is constant; the rim band shows the lower anchor. Conjecture: a curve exists ⇔ δ ≥ 0 and it pairs ≥ 0 with just these two anchors (verified against the actual diagram through order 63).
  • Above φ⁴ = (7+3√5)/2 ≈ 6.854 the lower anchor is always E, i.e. just m ≥ 0. Below φ⁴ the McDuff–Schlenk Fibonacci classes (5,1) = 2L, (13,2) = 5L, (34,5) = 13L, … own the lower side, accumulating at φ⁴.
  • Upper anchors: each even trunk (2n,1) owns a wide cone; interior fork-tree curves own slivers between trunks, refining forever near quadratic-irrational limits (e.g. around 55/6 inside [8,10]) — the dense region.
  • Every cone wall is itself a unicurve ray — the tree child of its two neighboring anchors — so the true decomposition has infinitely many cones. Hatched zones mark where they pile up below the current resolution; shrink the window there to expand them.
  • Sesquicurves (δ ≥ 1) mostly live on unicurve rays: same direction, other degrees d; the unicurve is the δ = 0 member of the column.

General sesqui constructions

A handful of general curve families — symbolic trees whose decorations are elementary functions of the class — together construct every conjectural sesquicurve tested. The page offers two views of the same catalogue: by interval (the first sub-tab: each owned ratio interval with just the families it actually needs — the same family can serve several intervals, re-centered at each interval’s own governing curve) and by family (one card per family: the exact regime of expected validity, the rigor status, and the evidence). Reference documents: sesqui_interval_owned_by_6_7_15_26.html (the interval owned by (6,1): two families, complete proofs — NEW 7/15), sesqui_interval_owned_by_8_7_14_26.html (the interval owned by 8: six families, complete proofs), tree_construction_8_interval_7_9_26.html and tree_construction_deep_intervals_7_10_26.html (working documents); comprehensive state of the art with full proofs and open problems: sesqui_8_10_state_of_the_art_7_10_26.html.

Conventions: a class is (p, q, d), gcd(p,q) = 1 — the conjectural rational sesquicuspidal curve of class dL − mE, m := 3d−p−q, with one (p,q)-cusp. The conjectural list (“window”) is: m ≥ 0, adjunction δ ≥ 0, and pairing ≥ 0 against every unicuspidal curve (equivalently the two anchors of the ratio interval). The families split at m = q: Family 1 covers m ≤ q outright; on [8,10], Families 2–7 partition m > q, while on the interval owned by (6,1) a single re-centered Family-5 tree covers all of m > q by itself (see “By interval”). Throughout τ4 = (7+3√5)/2 = 6.8541… (τ the golden ratio).

Trees are written as nested brackets: L1, L2, L3 are legs — unbounded edges lying on the corresponding line, with integer weights (3*L1 = one leg of weight 3) — and [a*X b*Y] is the vertex where the output edges of X and Y meet, consuming them with multiplicities a, b. A vertex whose two children are legs on two different lines is forced to sit at the intersection of those lines. One abbreviation is fixed once: Λk := [(k+1)*L1 k*L3] — the one-vertex tree with legs of weights k+1 and k at L₁∩L₃, which is exactly the tree of the (2k+4, 1)-unicuspidal curve (Λ₂ = (8,1), Λ₃ = (10,1), …). The one-vertex trees [L1 L2] and [L2 L3] (unit legs, at the other two intersections) are the trees of the (5,1)-curve and its counterpart. In the trees of Families 1–5 every vertex consumes at most one multi-vertex subtree, so the internal (bounded) edges form a single path ending at the output edge, each internal edge carrying the sum of the charges attached below it; Family 7’s canonical tree branches at its root (though its classes also admit single-path witnesses); Family 6 is the one case where branching is necessary. On the 8-interval the whole of m > q is now covered by a proved closed-form decision rule (see the Families 2–4 card); on the deeper intervals of [8,10] the same trees are exhaustively machine-verified.

Family 1  valid: 0 ≤ m ≤ q, p/q > τ4

Tree. [(q−m)·L₂ [(d−2q)·[L₂ L₃] (d−q)·L₁]] — the vertex [L₂ L₃] consumed d−2q times against a leg of weight d−q on L₁, then one leg on L₂ of weight exactly q−m.

Regime. Every conjectural class with 0 ≤ m ≤ q and p/q > τ4 — with no reference to the ratio interval: one formula for every owned interval of [8,10] at every nesting depth, and beyond both ends of the strip. This is possible because of the band theorem (proved, 2026-07-12 audit): no unicuspidal cap cuts into the band 0 ≤ m ≤ q for any p/q ≥ τ4 — so on it the conjectural list is simply {m ≥ 0, δ ≥ 0}, uniformly on every strip. In node count the band is the bottom slice of each column: δ − δmin ≤ q(p−3q)/9, δmin = (p²−7pq+q²+9)/18. The edge p/q = τ4 is genuine twice over: below it the middle vertex leaves the dense range (d > τ²q fails at m = 0), and δmin goes negative on the conic through the Fibonacci staircase.

Families 2–4  (m > q; together they partition the regime)

Every tree here uses p−2d copies of the vertex [L₁ L₂] together with vertices Λk at L₁∩L₃, whose legs carry total weights A = 3d−p−q on L₁ and B = d−2q on L₃; everything is consumed along a single path of internal edges. Set σ = A−B (= q − (p−2d)) and, when σ ≠ 0, let r be the remainder of min(A,B) mod |σ|. The three families below partition the regime by (σ, r) — every class falls in exactly one.

the proved decision rule (8-interval) — which tree for which class, no search

With x = p−2d, s = |σ|, e = B−3σ, k, r as above (all for 8 ≤ p/q < λ):

  1. σ > 0, r = 0: one vertex Λk (then k ≥ 3 automatically).
  2. σ > 0, r > 0, x ≤ 2e+5: [(s−1)*Λ3 [x*[L1 L2] Λe+3]] — this covers all k ≥ 4, since k ≥ 4 ⇒ x ≤ 2e+5.
  3. Otherwise k = 3 (and e = r). If Q₇(x,r) ≤ 1: the two-vertex split (Λ₃, Λ₄) with multiplicities (s−r, r).
  4. If Q₇(x,r) > 1 and 3x ≤ 2s+r: Family 5.
  5. If Q₇(x,r) > 1 and 3x > 2s+r: Family 7.
  6. σ < 0, r = 0: one weight-exchanged vertex.
  7. σ < 0, r > 0: [[{k+r}*L1 {k+r+1}*L3] [x*[L1 L2] (s−1)*[k*L1 (k+1)*L3]]] (the deeper vertex outside — the other order is provably unplaceable).
  8. σ = 0: [A*L1 A*L3] if A = 2i; otherwise [Λ3 [x*[L1 L2] [(A−4)*L1 (A−3)*L3]]].

Each case comes with a proof of admissibility and an explicit placement; the case split is exhaustive by arithmetic. Failure sets are exact: cases 4–5 occur precisely on {k=3, x>2r+5, Q₇(x,r)>1} (= the 327 classes at q ≤ 60 where every Family-2/3 split fails — verified as an exact set equality).

how to read the trees and figures

A tree consists of weighted legs (unbounded edges, each lying on one of the three lines), internal edges, and trivalent vertices; one edge is the output (red), whose direction is (−q, p−4q), the wall direction of the class. A vertex whose two children are legs on two different lines must sit at the intersection of those lines. Below: the one-vertex tree [L₁ L₂] (unit legs on L₁ and L₂, sitting at L₁∩L₂):

About this app

Created by Kyler Siegel with heavy AI assistance. There may be errors. Everything runs in your browser, in a single HTML file, with exact rational arithmetic (BigInt) — no floating point enters any mathematical computation.

What is being computed

You choose integer vectors v₁, …, v_k ∈ ℤ² and an order N. Each vector determines an initial wall: the line ℝv_i through the origin, carrying the function 1 + t_i z^(−v_i) (where z^(a,b) = xᵃyᵇ and t_i is a formal bookkeeping variable). This initial data is generally inconsistent: transporting a test monomial around a loop encircling the origin, applying each wall-crossing automorphism in turn, does not give the identity. By a theorem of Kontsevich–Soibelman / Gross–Siebert, there is an essentially unique way to add outgoing rays with correction functions so that the total monodromy becomes trivial — the consistent completion 𝒟∞. The app computes 𝒟∞ up to total degree < N in the t-variables and draws it: gray lines are the initial walls (labeled v₁, …, v_k, arrowheads pointing inward), colored rays are the outgoing walls, colored by the first order at which they acquire a nonzero function.

The computation is the classical KS algorithm (monodromy killing): order by order, compute the monodromy defect and add exactly the outgoing walls that cancel it. This is the same algorithm as the reference C++ implementation in this repository, against which this JS engine is cross-validated term by term.

Positive-only factorizations

Each outgoing ray in primitive direction d carries a power series. Rather than expanding it, the app displays it in the factored form used by Gräfnitz–Luo (and implicit in Gross–Pandharipande–Siebert):

f_d = ∏_α (1 + z^α)^c(α)

where α = (α₁,…,α_k) records how many times each t_i is used, the monomial is z^α = t₁^α₁⋯t_k^α_k · x^a y^b with (a,b) = −Σ α_i v_i, and the exponents c(α) are integers. Their significance: by positivity (GHKK) and the tropical correspondence (GPS), each c(α) is a count of tropical curves — all contributions are positive, nothing can cancel. So a factor is present if and only if at least one tree produces it, which is what makes this form the right one for existence arguments: exhibiting a single tree proves the term is there. Clicking a ray lists its factors; the badge shows c(α).

The trees (tree scattering)

Expanding a factor shows where its count comes from. Conceptually: deform the picture by translating each initial line to a general position (a fixed placement with exact rational offsets). The lines then cross at various points, each crossing scatters (a local KS computation), the produced rays cross other walls and scatter again, and so on. Each factor's coefficient decomposes as a sum over the resulting trees: at the chosen placement, every tree contributing to c(α) is found, and its weight is the product of local packet coefficients — each vertex's packet is the exact local scattering count of the walls meeting there (computed by the same KS engine, applied to the small local diagram, with wall multiplicities accounted for).

The checksum line Σ tree weights = c(α) ✓ under each factor is a genuine cross-validation: the coefficient was computed by the KS algorithm at the origin, the trees by the deformation method at the placement, and they must agree. When it shows ✓, the displayed tree list is provably complete — no tree missing, none spurious.

Two views are shown for each tree, tied together by shared vertex names (P₀, P₁, …):

  • In the plane — the tree drawn where it actually lives, at the fixed placement. Light gray chords are the initial lines: L_i denotes the translated copy of wall i, i.e. the line with direction v_i moved to its position in the placement (so v_i is a direction, L_i is a placed line, and the index always matches your input vectors). The vertex names P₀, P₁, … label the scattering points (crossing points at the placement), numbered in display order and shared between the two views. Green rays are the legs (the leaves of the tree): unbounded edges lying on an initial line, extending from their vertex in direction v_i, with the arrowhead at the vertex (the incoming momentum −v_i); a label like 2×L₁ is a leg of weight 2. Colored segments are intermediate curves, labeled by their charge α, with arrowheads at the vertex that consumes them. The bold arrow is the produced ray. At every vertex the edge directions balance (sum of incoming momenta = outgoing momentum). Parallel curves sharing the same support (e.g. a curve and its double cover) are drawn offset side by side.
  • As a tree — the same data drawn as a genuine tree (matching the figures of the tree-construction note): green boxes are the initial-line leaves, and each blue dot is one scattering vertex, annotated below with its local type (a₁,…,a_r) — the multiplicities consumed from its children, ordered left to right — plus, for two-ray vertices, det d = |m₁ ∧ m₂| of the incoming raw monomial directions (the index of the local scattering packet; no single determinant is defined at ≥ 3-ray vertices), then the resulting charge Σ a_j X_j and the packet weight wt … @vertex. Circled numbers on edges repeat consumption multiplicities > 1; the red arrow marks the output. When one packet admits several local decompositions at its vertex, its charge box hangs above the alternatives via dashed alt connectors — alternative readings whose contributions add, not extra tree edges.

A factor may have several trees rooted at different points (listed as separate blocks); their weights sum to c(α). The ⤢ expand button (or clicking the planar picture) opens both views fullscreen with zoom and pan.

The fundamental bijection and (p,q) coordinates

Enabling the (p,q) toggle in the header activates the fundamental bijection Q: a piecewise-linear identification ℤ² → ℤ²≥0 (with (1,0) ∼ (0,1)) used to convert wall/charge data into the (p,q) coordinates of the corresponding curves. It requires extra data: a vector v₀ ∈ ℤ² completing v₁,…,v_k to a fan (all consecutive cones spanning ≤ 180°; choose v₀ in the largest angular gap), and images u_i ∈ ℤ²≥0 for each v_i (v₀ itself maps to (1,0) ∼ (0,1)). Q is the unique map that is linear on each cone of the fan and sends v_i ↦ u_i.

With valid data, each factor row (and the fullscreen view) shows (p,q) = Q(m_α), where m_α = −Σ α_i v_i is the factor's monomial direction — note this uses the full (non-primitivized) vector, so multiple covers scale (p,q) accordingly. For the default F1 configuration the preset fan data is v₀=(0,1), u₁=(1,2), u₂=(1,1), u₃=(2,1), under which Q reproduces the F1 sesquicuspidal-curve dictionary (e.g. the charge (2,1,1) on the ray (−1,3) gets (p,q) = (7,1)). An invalid fan shows an error next to the toggle and simply suppresses the chips.

Mutations

The μ₁ … μ_k buttons (in the header — they act on the walls, so they don't need (p,q)) mutate the configuration at wall i: v_i ↦ −v_i, v_j ↦ v_j + max(v_j∧v_i, 0)·v_i, and the diagram is recomputed. When valid fan data is present it is transported to the neighboring toric model: v₀ by the same shear, u_j' = u_j for j ≠ i, and u_i' = Q(−v_i) — the unique rule making the (p,q) of every curve invariant: Q_new(μ_{v_i}(w)) = Q_old(w).

Involutivity: this mutation is not an involution on the nose at the level of the vectors — applying μ_i twice composes the two half-plane shears into the linear lattice map S(w) = w + (w∧v_i)·v_i, so v_j ↦ S(v_j) and v₀ ↦ S(v₀) (the entries grow), while all the u_j return exactly. The double-mutated data describes the same toric model in new lattice coordinates (Q'' = Q∘S⁻¹); the u's are intrinsic to the (p,q) side, which is why they are honestly involutive. For this reason pressing the same μ_i twice in a row in the app performs an exact undo (snapshot restore) rather than piling up sheared coordinates — matching the reference formalism, where mutation sequences exclude consecutive repeats. The armed button shows μ_i ↩ and its tooltip states this explicitly, since a user computing μ_i twice from the definition would get the sheared coordinates instead. Editing any input clears the undo memory. Fine print: if a mutation lands a wall ray exactly on v₀, the coincident rays share the identified boundary class and remain valid; a mutation is refused if it would produce genuinely non-integral fan data (all bijection arithmetic is exact integer arithmetic, so this is a real obstruction, not rounding), or if coordinates exceed the safe integer range after many mutations.

The δ invariant (cogenus)

Independently of the (p,q) toggle, every factor row shows δ, the cogenus of the corresponding curve: the number of double points the curve must carry by adjunction, beyond the contribution of its (p,q) cusp (so δ = 0 means unicuspidal). Geometrically: the charge α represents a curve in a non-toric blowup of a toric surface which meets the anticanonical boundary in a single point; performing corner blowups until the strict transform meets the boundary transversally at a smooth point removes every extra contribution of that intersection point — this resolved intersection is exactly the constant +1 below, and the full δ-weight of the (p,q) cusp is thereby discounted. (On F1 one checks the exact identity 2δ = (d−1)(d−2) − m(m−1) − (p−1)(q−1), i.e. δ = p_a − δ_cusp.) It is computed by the tropical self-intersection formula: reorder v₁,…,v_k (with their α_i) so that (v_out, v₁, …, v_k) is cyclically ordered counterclockwise, where v_out = −Σ α_i v_i; then

SI(α) = Σ_{i<j} α_i α_j (v_i ∧ v_j) − Σ_i α_i²,    2δ = 1 + SI(α).

For scanning across rays, the hover pill and the panel header list the ray's δ values succinctly (the set of values over its factors, with "+covers" when cover factors are present). With (p,q) and/or (d,m) enabled, the hover pill gains one extra line per toggle listing the distinct values over the ray's factors (capped at six, "+N more" beyond), and the panel header lists the distinct (d,m) classes on the ray in factor order. This is only established for primitive charges (v_out primitive): there the SI formula is invariant under corner blowups (added rays carry α = 0), so the computed value automatically equals the resolved-model one. For gcd(v_out) > 1 the boundary intersection cannot be resolved to a single unit contribution and no established formula exists — multiple covers are marked "cover" instead. For the F1 configuration δ agrees with the quiver Tits-form expression δ = (1 − q_Tits(α))/2, verified extensively in the reference computations. δ depends only on α and the v_i, not on the fan data u_i.

(d,m): the F1 curve class

For the exact configuration v₁=(1,0), v₂=(0,−1), v₃=(−1,−2) (in this order) a (d,m) toggle appears in the header: each factor is a curve of class dL − mE on F1 = Bl₁ℙ², with d = 2α₁ − α₃ and m = α₁ − α₂ (the verified F1 dictionary). Multiple covers scale the class accordingly. This is deliberately F1-specific: for other configurations the relevant surface has second homology of rank > 2 and no such two-parameter class description exists, so the toggle is hidden.

The toggle also remains available in every frame reached from F1 by mutations (the surface doesn't change; manual edits reset the eligibility). There the charge is base-mapped back to the F1 frame exactly and fed to the dictionary, using the charge transport rule: when viewing a curve in the model mutated at wall i, only α_i changes, by

α_i' = C·F_i − α_i,   C·F_i = Σ_{j≠i} [v_j ∧ v_i]₊ α_j + [m_α ∧ v_i]₊,   m_α = −Σ α_j v_j,

where C·F_i is the tropical intersection of the curve with the fiber line in direction v_i — note the output ray's term [m_α ∧ v_i]₊, whose omission is exactly the error in naive reflection formulas. The same formula in the new frame inverts it (α_i + α_i' = C·F_i). This rule is verified exhaustively: every factor of every mutated diagram matches the transported factor of the original with the same coefficient, forward and backward, including composed mutations — and the resulting (d,m) agrees with an independent intrinsic reconstruction from (p, q, δ) wherever the latter is defined. The rare "—" appears only for factors with no charge presentation in the original frame (mutated-exceptional echoes).

Conventions and fine print

  • The cutoff is strict: only charges with |α| = Σα_i < N are computed.
  • The factor (1 + t_i z^(−v_i)) appearing on the ray in direction −v_i is the continuation (echo) of the initial line through the origin — it has no scattering trees.
  • The placement used for the trees is fixed (seeded). The set of trees genuinely depends on the placement — trees rebalance as the lines move — but the sums do not. What you see is the tree decomposition at one honest choice.
  • Vertices where three or more walls happen to meet at the placement are handled exactly (they are unavoidable: some concurrences occur at every placement, and their mixed local packets carry real contributions).
  • Vertex positions are exact rationals; floating point is used only to put pixels on the screen.

F₁ exchange graph tab

The F₁ exchange graph tab BFS-generates the mutation graph of toric models of F₁, starting from the standard model v₁,v₂,v₃ = (1,0), (0,−1), (−1,−2), v₀ = (0,1), u = (1,2), (1,1), (2,1), using exactly the μi rule of the header buttons. Two models count as the same seed when they differ by a simultaneous SL(2,ℤ) change of the v-side lattice and a relabeling of the indices 1…3 (u’s compared exactly, up to the boundary identification (t,0) ~ (0,t)); on seeds, μi² is then a genuine involution (the v’s shear, the u’s return on the nose).

Identification. Setting Bij = vi∧vj, the start model is the acyclic 3-point quiver with arrow multiplicities {a,b,c} = {2,1,1}, and on every edge of the graph the toric mutation transforms these wedges by exactly the Fomin–Zelevinsky rule (checked at build time). The seed graph is therefore the exchange graph Γ(Q) for a = 2, b = c = 1 — Figure 10.3 of M. Warkentin, Exchange Graphs via Quiver Mutation (TU Chemnitz, 2014; PDF in this folder) — with strip arrow numbers a, a+1, a(a+1)−1 = 2, 3, 5.

Dictionary.

  • Each 2-face (region) of the figure carries a single u-value: μi changes only ui, so the u’s play the role of the cluster variables / tilting summands that label Warkentin’s regions.
  • The thick horizontal line is the canonical transjective chain = the acyclic toric models.
  • Pentagon faces = the regions 1, 2, P₁, P₂, I₁, I₂ and their τ-shifts; ∞-gons = 3, P₃, I₃ (and τ-shifts), the boundary-class region ∂ = (1,0) ~ (0,1) (the unlabeled bottom-centre region of Fig 10.3), and the regular (non-transjective) regions such as (6,1). Region names are derived from the transjective entry order along the chain together with the vertex dictionary v₁↔1, v₂↔3, v₃↔2 (matching incident multiplicities); τ acts as the glide reflection, which on u-values is literally (p,q) ↔ (q,p).
  • Forks (mutation-cyclic seeds whose largest arrow multiplicity is strict) hang in complete binary trees, one per pentagon, rooted at the cyclic strip seed with arrow multiset {1,2,3}; their arrow triples run through the Markov-like tree of x²+y²+z²−xyz = 8 rooted at {2,3,5} — the F₁-analogue of the Markov tree for P². Every solution triple appears once per tree (plus mirrors), so a given triple such as {2,5,7} = ((1,10),(1,1),(1,8)) sits one generation inside each tree.

Verification. Everything is recomputed in your browser when the tab is first opened, with exact integer arithmetic: the mutations themselves are performed in BigInt (no floating-point ceiling), the strip is explored to distance 16 and each fork tree to generation 4 (beyond that the trees provably continue as complete binary trees — Warkentin’s Lemma 2.5 — while their entries grow doubly exponentially, so they are truncated by design and drawn with continuation dashes). Wherever the engine’s Number arithmetic is provably exact (all coordinates below √2⁵³) it is cross-checked edge-by-edge against the BigInt mutation. 3-regularity, μi² = id on seeds, the Fomin–Zelevinsky rule on wedges, and the Markov-type invariant x²+y²+z²∓xyz ≡ 8 are all asserted, and any failure is displayed in red under the figure. The standalone referee (same computation at depth 9 plus the full written report) is exchange_graph_F1_7_3_26.mjs in this folder.

Patterns. Transjective u-values satisfy un+1 = 6un − un−1 within each of the three families (growth ratio 3+2√2); the exchange relation is uold + unew = Σ mji·uj over one side (in- or out-arrows) of the mutated vertex, e.g. (1,2)+(4,1) = (1,1)+2·(2,1); and |ui∧uj| equals the arrow multiplicity |vi∧vj| for exactly two of the three pairs at every seed (the failing pair is the one whose cone crosses the v₀-ray).

F₁ anchor fan tab

The anchor fan shows where the condition “C ⋅res U ≥ 0 for every unicurve U” reduces to just two inequalities. A ray direction fixes the contact pair (p,q); the curves on that ray form a column dL−mE with m = 3d−P−Q and d free, and every pairing is linear in d. Each unicurve is intrinsically upper-type (3m > d: its inequality reads d ≤ …) or lower-type (3m < d: it reads d ≥ …), so the all-unicurves filter is a d-interval:

max(lower bounds)  ≤  d  ≤  min(upper bounds).

The main (upper) anchor of a cone is the unicurve achieving the min — the single interesting curve (“the anchor 6” on ratios just above 6, (8,1) on [8, λ], etc.). The lower anchor achieves the max; it is usually the boundary class E, whose pairing is just m, i.e. the trivial constraint m ≥ 0 — this is why “one curve + m ≥ 0” suffices on windows like [8,10]. But below φ⁴ = (7+3√5)/2 ≈ 6.854 (dashed magenta; the McDuff–Schlenk constant) genuine curves beat m ≥ 0 from below — e.g. (13,2) = 5L near ratio 6.4, where its necessity is confirmed in the actual diagram at order 25 — walking the odd-index Fibonacci staircase (5,1), (13,2), (34,5), (89,13), … up to φ⁴. The wheel shows the intersected cones: each has a unique (upper, lower) anchor pair, and matches one row of the collapsible table. The two rim bands show each side’s pure decomposition on its own — inner band = upper anchors (labels at the wheel bisectors group the intersected cones into their upper families), outer band = lower anchors (gray = E, i.e. just m ≥ 0). The two decompositions are independent with unrelated breakpoints, which is exactly why the wheel is finer than either band. On a cone the conjectural existence criterion for a primitive charge is: δ ≥ 0 plus nonnegative pairing with the two anchors.

τ is the symmetry of (F₁, N) swapping the two branches of the node: it fixes curve classes and transposes contact pairs, (p,q) ↔ (Q,P). The left half of the picture is the chamber side (s = p/q > 3), the right half its τ-mirror; mirror cones are labelled by the transposed pair (e.g. (1,4) opposite (4,1) — the same curve 2L−E, tangent to the other branch). Both halves converge to the vertical ray of E.

Rays. Blue rays are unicurves (δ = 0), drawn from the mutation family rather than the diagram — the BFS is exact (coordinates capped at 2²⁶ ≈ 67 million so double arithmetic is provably exact) and reaches far higher degree than any KS computation; thick blue = currently an anchor. By default only anchors and shallow curves are drawn (the “uni” and “sesqui” checkboxes show/hide the two families; the “show unis” selector adds non-anchor unicurves by their order P − 3Q — the t-degree of the curve’s term, so “order ≤ 15” means the curves visible in a modest diagram computation; anchors are always drawn — all display-only, no recompute). The window control restricts the sweep to a ratio range (e.g. 8..10): the direction count scales with the window width, so narrow windows admit far higher resolution Q (up to 1500) and expose the deep self-similar cascades; the view auto-fits the window's wedge and pale flanks mark the excluded ranges. Note that sesquicurves mostly live on the same rays as unicurves: the direction fixes (p,q), the δ = 0 member of the column (if realized) is the unicurve, and the δ ≥ 1 members at other degrees d are sesquicurves — hover any blue ray to see them listed; an orange dot at the rim of a blue ray marks exactly these shared directions. Full orange rays mark the directions that carry δ ≥ 1 primitive terms but no unicurve at all. Raising the sesqui order (the cap is just compute time — the KS run is exact at any order) adds both more dots and more sesqui-only rays. Multiple covers are excluded throughout. Hovering a cone or band highlights its anchor rays; clicking pins the data below the figure; the collapsible table lists the full refined decomposition.

Caveats: the decomposition is computed at finite resolution (all coprime directions with denominator ≤ Qmax) from a finite unicurve family (mutation depth D); a family of depth D only contains the trunk curves (2n,1) up to roughly n ≈ D+1, so the far tail of the fan is truncated, and near the accumulation ratios (quadratic irrationals) the true fan refines forever — deeper slivers appear as you raise the depth and resolution. The sector walls are themselves unicurve rays (verified in the standalone referee anchor_decomposition_7_3_26.mjs, which this tab ports); each wall is the tree child of its two adjacent owners. Symmetries: the Orevkov twists (McDuff–Siegel)

The pair (F₁, N) carries birational symmetries beyond the branch swap τ: the generalized Orevkov twists Φ, Ψ of McDuff–Siegel (Geiser-type involution compositions in Kollár’s description). For F₁ (K = 6): Φ sends a (p,q)-well-placed curve to (6p−q, p), and Ψ sends it to (q+6(p−6q), p−6q) for p/q > 6. On the exchange graph these act as automorphisms realized by mutation words: τ is the mirror, Φ is the glide reflection (the strip translation / AR translate — the u-chain recurrence uₖ₊₁ = 6uₖ − uₖ₋₁ is Φ), and together they generate an infinite dihedral symmetry group. The three Φ-orbits of (1,1), (2,1), (4,1) are the three strands of the F₁ ellipsoid-embedding staircase, accumulating at 3+2√2 ≈ 5.828 (the transjective cascade of the anchor fan); Ψ folds the ratio line at 7 and maps the trunks (2n,1) to the descending lower cascade (13,2), (25,4), (37,6), … while Φ maps them to the ascending one (35,6), (47,8), (59,10), … — so the lower-anchor structure at 6 is exactly the twist orbit of the trunk family. On the m = 0 classes (pullbacks from ℂℙ²) the relevant twist is Φ₇ (K = 7), whose orbits of (5,1) and (13,2) are the McDuff–Schlenk Fibonacci classes accumulating at φ⁴. The symmetry stepper in the exchange graph tab walks all three generators: colored markers start on the base regions 1, 2, 3 (click any region to add or remove markers) and each ▶ press applies the symmetry to all markers in tandem (◀ steps back) — Φ marches along the strip (chamber-oriented regions move by (p,q) ↦ (6p−q, p), mirror-oriented ones by the τ-conjugate glide), while Ψ and τ are involutions, so their markers bounce between partners (Ψ pairs the P-chain with the base/I-chain — the Geiser reflection). Markers whose image lies beyond the drawn strip park with an ✕; the view expands to follow the markers. The twists do NOT relate the even strips [8,10], [10,12], … to each other — those are only combinatorially isomorphic (identical fork-tree quivers).
