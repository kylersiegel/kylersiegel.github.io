Stabilized Ellipsoid Embedding Function

The function \(c_b(x)\) is the infimal \(c\) such that \(E(1,x) \times \mathbb{R}^2\) symplectically embeds into \(\bigl(\mathbb{CP}^2(c)\;\#\;\overline{\mathbb{CP}}^2(cb)\bigr) \times \mathbb{R}^2\). Each sesquicuspidal curve \(C\) in class \(d[L]-m[E]\) with tangency type \((p,q)\) gives \(c_b(p/q) \ge \frac{p}{d - bm}\).

Mathematical background

We consider \(\mathbb{CP}^2\;\#\;\overline{\mathbb{CP}}^2\) (the one-point blowup of \(\mathbb{CP}^2\), also called \(F_1\)) with symplectic form such that the line class \([L]\) has area \(1\) and the exceptional class \([E]\) has area \(b\), where \(0 \le b < 1\).

A \((p,q)\)-sesquicuspidal curve \(C\) in homology class \(d[L]-m[E]\) gives the lower bound \[c_b\!\left(\tfrac{p}{q}\right) \;\ge\; \frac{p}{d - bm},\] where \(d - bm\) is the symplectic area of \(C\).

The conjecture is that \(c_b(x)\) is the smallest function \([1,\infty) \to \mathbb{R}_{>0}\) that is (1) continuous, (2) nondecreasing, (3) \(c_b(x)/x\) is nonincreasing, and (4) satisfies all sesquicurve bounds. Equivalently, \[c_b(x) \;=\; \sup_C \min\!\Bigl(\frac{q}{d-bm}\,x,\;\frac{p}{d-bm}\Bigr)\] where the supremum is over all sesquicurves \(C\).

Existence conjecture for sesquicuspidal curves

The list of sesquicuspidal curves used here is itself conjectural. It relies on the intersection conjecture: a primitive tuple \((\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3)\) with coprime \((p,q)\) and \(\delta > 0\) represents an actual sesquicuspidal curve if and only if it has nonnegative intersection number with every unicuspidal curve (\(\delta = 0\)). This was verified against the true scattering diagram (order 60) with zero counterexamples.

The unicuspidal curves themselves are computed exactly via BFS of the scattering mutation graph (piecewise-linear mutations on triples of \(\mathbb{Z}^2\) vectors). The sesquicuspidal curves are then enumerated by filtering primitive tuples through the intersection criterion. Two additional "degree-1" curves (the line \([L]\) and the fiber \([L]-[E]\)) are added manually, as they correspond to the trivial monomial in the scattering diagram.

Data: from scattering diagram computations for \(F_1\) with walls \((1,0)\), \((0,-1)\), \((-1,-2)\).

Conjectural folding curve

For large \(x\), the function \(c_b(x)\) appears to converge to a smooth curve: \[c_b(x) \;\sim\; \begin{cases} \dfrac{3x}{x+1} & \text{if } b \le \tfrac{1}{3}, \\[8pt] \dfrac{2x}{(1-b)\,x + 2b} & \text{if } b \ge \tfrac{1}{3}. \end{cases}\] The two formulas agree at \(b = \frac{1}{3}\). The limiting values are \(c_b(\infty) = 3\) for \(b \le \frac{1}{3}\) and \(c_b(\infty) = \frac{2}{1-b}\) for \(b \ge \frac{1}{3}\).

For \(b \le \frac{1}{3}\), the asymptotic is governed by curves in class \(d[L]\) (avoiding \([E]\)). For \(b > \frac{1}{3}\), it is governed by curves in class \(n[L]-(n{-}1)[E]\) with near-maximal exceptional multiplicity. The transition at \(b = \frac{1}{3}\) is where the "cost" of the exceptional class begins to matter.
